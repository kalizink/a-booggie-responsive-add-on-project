Why A Boogie With da Hoodie is the best

His most recent album, Hoodie SZN, spent two weeks atop the Billboard 200—a milestone for the homegrown rapper from the Bronx’s Highbridge neighborhood. “I know it’s like I’m supposed to be all excited, but that’s not me,” he says in between bites of a cheese puff. With hundreds of songs recorded and at the ready and a growing number of soundalikes on his heels, he feels that this is no time to celebrate for too long: “I give myself a pat on the back, then keep on pushing.”

Though he would never give himself the title, an argument could be made that A Boogie is the current holder of rap’s contested “King of New York” title—at least for his generation of artists.

In the past five years, he’s ascended the ranks from a random rapper on SoundCloud to one of the most influential stylists in NY rap. Look no further than the typical New York City public school kid’s playlist for evidence of his reach, or to the music coming from up-and-comers like fellow Bronx denizen Lil Tjay, A$AP Mob’s Smooky MarGielaa, and Brooklyn’s Jay Gwuapo for evidence of his influence. It would be easy to accuse these rappers of biting, but the trained ear of young New York rap fans can hear the subtle differences between these artists who combine singing and rapping, and who often employ Auto-Tune to wax poetic about heartbreak and street life over melancholy piano keys and a booming low end.
